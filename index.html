Healthy Terms

  1. BMI: Body mass index (BMI) is an estimate of the bodyâ€™s fat content, calculated from measurements of height and weight and are used to determine if your weight is healthy. A healthy BMI is 24.9.

  2. Waist measurements: Carrying excess body fat around your middle is more of a health risk than if weight is on your hips and thighs. Waist circumference is a better estimate of visceral fat, the dangerous internal fat that coats the organs. It is therefore a more accurate predictor of cardiovascular, type 2 diabetes, cancer and stroke risks.

  3. Women waist measurements: Measurements greater than 35 inches increases risk of health problems even if at a healthy weight.

  4. Men waist measurements: Measurements greater than 40 inches increases risk of health problems even if at a healthy weight.

  5. BMR: Basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the rate at which the body uses energy while at rest to keep vital functions going, such as breathing and keeping warm.

  6. TDEE: Total Daily Energy Expenditure(TDEE) is the total amount of energy that the body burns with corresponding activity level.

  7. Activity Level: BMR is multiplied by corresponding activity level to determine TDEE(activity level in parenthesis).
    • Sedentary- People who work desk jobs and engage in very little exercise or chores.(1.2)
    • Lightly active - People who do chores and go on long walks/engage in exercise at least 1 to 3 days in a week.(1.375)
    • Moderately active - People who move a lot during the day and workout (moderate effort) at least 3 to 5 days in a week. (1.55)
    • Very active - People who play sports or engage in vigorous exercise on most days.(1.725)
    • Extra active - People who do intense workouts 6 to 7 days a week with work that demands physical activity.(1.9)

  8. When trying to lose/gain weight, a general rule of thumb is to reduce/increase your calorie intake by 500 calories than your body needs (TDEE) to maintain your current weight. This will help you lose/gain about 1 pound (0.45 kg) of body weight per week.

  9. Water: Water is your body's principal chemical component and makes up about 50% to 70% of your body weight. Your body depends on water to survive. The U.S. National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine determined that an adequate daily fluid intake is:
    • 15.5 cups (3.7 liters) of fluids a day for men
    • 11.5 cups (2.7 liters) of fluids a day for women
